My role, system overview, and key subsystems

My role

For nearly a year, I held the position of Chief Engineer at Delft Hyperloop. Around December, I also assumed the planning and execution responsibilities from our project manager, who was temporarily unable to continue in his role.

In this combined capacity, I was responsible for both the technical direction and project coordination of the team. My main tasks included:

  • Defining technical objectives and requirements — from high-level system goals down to detailed component specifications.
  • Coordinating work across departments, ensuring alignment of parameters, design interfaces, and dependencies between subsystems.
  • Leading planning and execution across different time horizons:
    • long-term (annual roadmap and milestones),
    • medium-term (project phases), and
    • short-term (daily operational priorities).
  • Overseeing design quality, production, and testing processes to ensure consistency and technical excellence.
  • Managing cross-functional communication, monitoring interfaces between technical teams to prevent integration issues.
  • Leading departmental leads at a strategic level while also supervising the daily operations of the testing team later in the year.

This role required a balance of strategic oversight and hands-on technical leadership, as well as close collaboration with multiple engineering disciplines to deliver a cohesive and high-performing prototype.

System and Objectives

Delft Hyperloop is a team that each year develops a new hyperloop prototype from the ground up to set new records and compete in international challenges. In my year, we set a goal of carrying out a first levitating lane switch without moving elements — a crucial step toward a scalable and low-maintenance hyperloop network. This design enabled maximum throughput while eliminating the wear and reliability challenges associated with moving track components.

Levitation, Propulsion and Structure

The pod achieved full electromagnetic levitation, even during lane switching and turning maneuvers. Vertical stability was provided by four Hybrid Electromagnetic Suspension (HEMS) units, while lateral guidance was maintained through four Electromagnetic Suspension (EMS) actuators that dynamically balanced forces to keep the pod centered within millimeter precision. Propulsion was delivered by a Linear Flux Switching Permanent Magnet (LFSPM) motor — an efficient, track-passive design that enabled acceleration up to 1 m/s². The lightweight rectangular ladder-frame chassis integrated both the propulsion and braking systems, with symmetrical brakes mounted near the vertical beams to ensure optimal stability.

Thermal Management, Control Systems and Powertrain

To ensure vacuum compatibility and sustained performance, the pod incorporated a water-based thermal management system that extracted heat from the motor drives and vacuum enclosure and dissipated it through evaporative cooling. We achieved high-precision localization, accurate to 3 mm, using a scalable strip-laser system, while levitation sensors maintained positioning within ±10 µm. A set of safety and communication PCBs continuously monitored pod health, activating braking in case of faults, and constant telemetry ensured reliable communication with the ground station. The team additionally developed fully custom high- and low-voltage batteries as well as the power distribution boards.
